## Representation and Features

### 1. Why can a strong backbone still fail on a downstream task with enough data?

A strong backbone — say a ViT-L pretrained on ImageNet-22k or a CLIP backbone — learns features that are broadly discriminative but not necessarily aligned with what the downstream task needs. Pretraining objectives shape the feature manifold: a classification backbone learns global semantic features, but a downstream task like dense depth estimation or small-object detection may need fine-grained local geometry, high-resolution spatial detail, or features that discriminate between visually similar classes the backbone never saw. The backbone's internal feature distribution reflects its pretraining domain, and when the downstream domain has different correlations (e.g., overhead imagery vs. ImageNet), the "strong" features can actively mislead the head.

Even with plenty of downstream data, the backbone's architecture imposes an inductive bias on what information survives the forward pass. A backbone that aggressively downsamples (stride-32 outputs) loses spatial precision that matters for segmentation or keypoint tasks. Its learned attention patterns or receptive fields may focus on textures and shapes that are irrelevant to the new domain. End-to-end fine-tuning can partially recover, but if the backbone is very large relative to the downstream dataset, you risk slow convergence or destabilizing early layers while the head has not yet learned a useful gradient signal.

The practical lesson: "strong backbone" is always relative to a task distribution. A backbone is a compressed representation of its pretraining data; if the downstream task requires information that was discarded during that compression, no amount of head training recovers it. This is why architecture-task alignment (e.g., FPN necks for detection, high-resolution branches for segmentation) often matters more than raw backbone ImageNet accuracy.

### 2. When do frozen foundation-model features beat end-to-end fine-tuning?

Frozen features win when the downstream dataset is small relative to the model's capacity, the pretraining distribution covers the downstream domain reasonably well, and the task can be solved with a relatively simple head on top of rich features. In this regime, fine-tuning risks overfitting the backbone to the small dataset, destroying the generalizable representations learned during pretraining. Linear probing or lightweight adapter heads on frozen features act as a strong regularizer — you are effectively constraining the hypothesis space to linear (or near-linear) functions of the pretrained representation.

Frozen features also win when you need fast iteration, multi-task deployment, or memory efficiency. If you serve one backbone across ten tasks, freezing it lets you cache features and train only small heads. For CLIP or DINOv2 features, the zero-shot transfer is often competitive enough that the engineering cost of fine-tuning is not justified. The key diagnostic: if a linear probe on frozen features already gets within a few points of the fine-tuned model, you are in the regime where freezing is the better engineering choice.

Fine-tuning wins when there is a significant domain gap (e.g., medical imaging, satellite, radar), when the task requires the backbone to restructure its internal representations (e.g., learning depth-aware features for monocular 3D detection), or when you have enough data to regularize the fine-tuning process. The middle ground — freezing early layers and fine-tuning later layers, or using LoRA/adapter methods — often gives the best of both worlds. The decision is fundamentally about the bias-variance tradeoff: freezing adds bias (you are stuck with pretraining features) but reduces variance; fine-tuning reduces bias but increases variance.

### 3. What are the trade-offs between CNN, ViT, and hybrid backbones for robotics perception?

CNNs bring strong local inductive biases — translation equivariance, local connectivity, and hierarchical feature extraction — that make them data-efficient and fast to converge on structured visual tasks. For robotics, where data budgets are often modest (real-robot data is expensive), CNNs like ResNet or ConvNeXt remain competitive. They also have well-understood latency profiles: depthwise-separable convolutions, known memory access patterns, and mature TensorRT optimization paths. On edge devices (Jetson, Orin), CNN latency is highly predictable.

ViTs excel when pretraining data is abundant and the task benefits from global context — for instance, understanding scene layout, long-range spatial relationships, or reasoning about occlusions. ViTs with patch embeddings treat the image as a sequence, giving every patch the ability to attend to every other patch from the first layer. This is powerful but expensive: self-attention is O(n^2) in the number of patches, and ViTs are notoriously data-hungry without heavy pretraining or strong augmentation (DeiT-style training). For robotics, the latency cost of full self-attention can be prohibitive at high resolutions or high frame rates.

Hybrid architectures (e.g., EfficientFormer, CoAtNet, Swin Transformer) aim to get the best of both: convolutions in early stages for efficient local feature extraction and reduced sequence length, then attention in later stages for global reasoning. For robotics perception specifically, the choice depends on the deployment constraint: if you need 30+ FPS on an edge GPU with deterministic latency, a CNN or lightweight hybrid is usually right. If you are running BEV fusion on a powerful compute platform (like AV stacks with A100s), a ViT backbone can leverage large-scale pretraining for better feature quality. The key practical consideration is not just accuracy but latency variance — attention mechanisms can have input-dependent compute costs that make worst-case latency hard to bound, which is dangerous in safety-critical real-time systems.

### 4. How do positional encodings affect dense vision tasks?

Positional encodings inject spatial structure into architectures (particularly transformers) that otherwise treat their inputs as unordered sets. For dense prediction tasks — segmentation, detection, depth estimation — spatial relationships are the core of the problem, so the choice of positional encoding directly affects what the model can learn. Absolute sinusoidal or learned positional encodings (as in the original ViT) tie features to fixed grid positions, which makes them resolution-dependent and brittle to test-time resolution changes. If you train at 224x224 and deploy at 512x512, absolute positional encodings must be interpolated, which degrades performance.

Relative positional encodings (as in Swin Transformer's relative position bias) encode the offset between tokens rather than their absolute position. This is more natural for dense tasks because spatial relationships are inherently relative: "this pixel is 3 positions to the right of that pixel" is more useful than "this pixel is at position (47, 112)." Relative encodings generalize better across resolutions and are critical for tasks like detection where objects can appear anywhere. Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE), adapted from NLP, encode position through rotation in the query-key dot product, providing a smooth decay of attention with distance — a useful inductive bias for spatial tasks.

For dense prediction in the autonomy/robotics setting, the most important practical effect is resolution generality. Camera rigs change, crop strategies vary, and multi-scale feature pyramids operate at different spatial resolutions. Architectures with relative or convolutional positional encodings (e.g., using depthwise convolutions as position encoding, as in many modern ViTs) adapt gracefully. Absolute encodings force you to either retrain or accept interpolation artifacts. The conditional position encoding (CPE) approach — injecting position information via a small convolution on the value or between transformer blocks — has become popular precisely because it adapts to arbitrary input sizes while being architecturally simple.

### 5. When do geometric priors still matter even with large pretraining?

Geometric priors — camera intrinsics/extrinsics, epipolar geometry, projective constraints, rigid-body motion models — remain essential when the task requires metric accuracy rather than just semantic correctness. A foundation model pretrained on internet images can learn that "this is a car" and even approximate "the car is far away," but it cannot learn your specific camera's focal length, your sensor's extrinsic calibration, or the precise metric relationship between pixel coordinates and 3D world coordinates. These are deployment-specific constants that must be injected as priors, not learned from data.

In multi-view geometry tasks (stereo, structure-from-motion, BEV projection), geometric priors constrain the solution space in ways that data alone cannot efficiently learn. Epipolar constraints in stereo matching reduce a 2D search to a 1D search along epipolar lines — no amount of data makes it efficient to learn this constraint implicitly. Similarly, in BEV perception (LSS, BEVFormer), the lift-splat operation uses known camera geometry to project image features into 3D — attempting to learn this projection end-to-end would require vastly more data and would fail on new camera configurations.

Even for tasks where semantic understanding dominates (like object detection), geometric priors help in the long tail. A pretrained model might struggle with objects at extreme distances or unusual aspect ratios, but knowing the camera's field of view and the ground plane equation lets you constrain expected object sizes at each image location. In robotics, where you control the sensor configuration, injecting geometric priors is essentially free — you have the calibration data — and it provides guarantees that learned features cannot: a projective transformation is exact, not approximate. The hybrid approach — learned features for semantics, explicit geometry for metric reasoning — consistently outperforms pure end-to-end approaches in safety-critical perception.

### 6. How would you explain domain shift in feature space rather than only in pixel space?

Domain shift is often discussed in terms of pixel-level statistics — different lighting, weather, camera sensors — but the deeper and more actionable framing is in feature space. A trained model maps images to intermediate feature representations, and domain shift means the feature distributions P_source(z) and P_target(z) diverge. This divergence can happen even when pixel-level statistics look similar: two datasets with identical brightness histograms can produce very different feature distributions if the semantic content, object co-occurrence patterns, or spatial layouts differ. Conversely, large pixel-level differences (e.g., synthetic vs. real) can produce aligned feature distributions if the model has learned invariant features.

The feature-space view is more useful because it directly explains classifier failure. A linear decision boundary learned on source features will misclassify target examples if those examples land in different regions of feature space — even if they look reasonable to a human in pixel space. Domain adaptation methods like DANN (Domain-Adversarial Neural Networks) operate explicitly on this principle: they add a domain classifier head and use gradient reversal to encourage the backbone to produce domain-invariant features. Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) and CORAL losses similarly minimize distributional distances in feature space.

Practically, you can diagnose feature-space shift by extracting features from both domains and computing metrics like: (a) the Frechet distance between Gaussian-fitted feature distributions (analogous to FID), (b) t-SNE/UMAP visualizations showing cluster separation by domain, (c) linear probing accuracy on domain classification using intermediate features — if you can easily tell domains apart from features, the shift is severe. For autonomy, this framing is critical: a model trained on daytime suburban driving might have pixel distributions that overlap with nighttime highway driving (both have dark regions, lane markings), but the feature representations for "road boundary" or "pedestrian" may be completely different. Monitoring feature-space statistics at deployment time (via lightweight distribution tests on activations) is a practical way to detect silent domain shift before it causes downstream failures.

## Detection and Localization

### 1. Compare one-stage and two-stage detectors. Where does each still win?

Two-stage detectors (Faster R-CNN, Cascade R-CNN) first generate region proposals, then classify and refine each proposal. This decoupled design gives the second stage a focused, cropped feature representation per candidate, which is particularly powerful for: (a) small objects, where the RoI pooling/align step extracts dedicated features at an appropriate scale rather than relying on a single feature map cell, (b) high-precision localization, where the second stage can iteratively refine box coordinates (Cascade R-CNN chains multiple refinement stages), and (c) complex classification, where per-RoI features can support heavier heads. Two-stage detectors still dominate benchmarks like LVIS (long-tail, many classes) and tasks requiring very precise localization (e.g., sub-pixel keypoint detection).

One-stage detectors (YOLO family, SSD, RetinaNet, FCOS) predict boxes and classes directly from dense feature maps in a single pass. Their advantage is speed and architectural simplicity: no RoI pooling, no two-pass computation, better suited for real-time deployment. RetinaNet showed that the accuracy gap was largely due to class imbalance (solved by focal loss), not architectural inferiority. Modern one-stage detectors (YOLOv8, RT-DETR) have closed the accuracy gap significantly by incorporating multi-scale feature fusion (FPN/PAN), better label assignment strategies (SimOTA, TAL), and decoupled classification/regression heads.

For autonomy and robotics, the choice is often dictated by latency budgets. If you need 30+ FPS on edge hardware, one-stage detectors are the practical choice. If you are running on a powerful GPU with a 100ms latency budget and need maximal accuracy on rare, small objects (e.g., distant pedestrians, debris on the road), two-stage detectors still offer measurable advantages. DETR-family models (which are technically one-stage but use learned queries and Hungarian matching) represent a third path that eliminates NMS entirely and shows strong results on large objects but historically struggles with small objects — though recent variants (DINO-DETR, Co-DETR) have largely addressed this.

### 2. Explain anchor-based versus anchor-free detection and when each breaks.

Anchor-based detectors (Faster R-CNN, SSD, RetinaNet) predefine a set of reference boxes (anchors) at each spatial location with various aspect ratios and scales. The model predicts offsets from these anchors and classifies each as object/background. The anchor design encodes prior knowledge about expected object shapes — which helps when object sizes are known and relatively consistent (e.g., cars, pedestrians on roads). However, anchors introduce hyperparameters (sizes, ratios, number per location) that require tuning per dataset, and poorly chosen anchors create a mismatch: if no anchor has sufficient IoU with a ground-truth box, that object becomes hard to detect. Anchor-based methods also produce a massive number of candidate predictions (e.g., ~100k anchors), most of which are negative, creating a severe class imbalance that requires careful handling (focal loss, OHEM).

Anchor-free detectors (FCOS, CenterNet, CornerNet) predict object properties directly — typically a center point and distances to box edges (FCOS) or center heatmap plus size regression (CenterNet). This eliminates anchor hyperparameters and naturally handles objects of unusual shapes. FCOS assigns each feature map location to a ground-truth box if it falls inside the box, and predicts the four distances (l, t, r, b) to box edges. This is conceptually simpler and avoids the combinatorial explosion of anchors. However, anchor-free methods can struggle with: (a) ambiguity when multiple objects overlap at the same spatial location (the center-assignment problem), solved partially by FPN-level assignment in FCOS, and (b) large-scale variation, where regressing very large distances to box edges is numerically less stable than predicting small offsets from a well-sized anchor.

In practice, modern detectors have blurred this distinction. YOLOv8 is anchor-free. DETR uses learned object queries instead of spatial anchors. The label assignment strategy (how you decide which predictions are responsible for which ground-truth boxes) has become more important than anchor vs. anchor-free: dynamic assignment methods like SimOTA and Task-Aligned Assignment (TAL) jointly consider classification confidence and localization quality, making the anchor design less critical. Anchor-based methods still have an edge when you have strong prior knowledge about object geometry (e.g., fixed-camera traffic monitoring where car sizes are predictable), while anchor-free methods are preferred when object shapes vary widely or when simplicity and fewer hyperparameters are valued.

### 3. What limits small-object detection, and what knobs actually move the needle?

Small-object detection is fundamentally limited by feature resolution. After several stages of striding and pooling, a 16x16 pixel object on a stride-32 backbone is represented by a fraction of a single feature cell — there simply is not enough spatial information to distinguish the object from background or classify it accurately. The information bottleneck is spatial: small objects have few pixels, and each downsampling layer discards spatial detail. Additionally, small objects have lower signal-to-noise ratio in training: they contribute less to the loss (fewer positive pixels/anchors), their box regression targets are more sensitive to small absolute errors (a 2-pixel shift is negligible for a 200-pixel box but catastrophic for a 16-pixel box), and they are disproportionately affected by annotation noise.

The knobs that actually help: (a) **Higher-resolution inputs** — the single most reliable improvement; doubling resolution approximately quadruples the feature area for small objects. (b) **Feature Pyramid Networks** with early-stage detection — using P2 or P3 features (stride 4–8) for small objects, though this is computationally expensive. (c) **Dilated/deformable convolutions** in the backbone to maintain spatial resolution without reducing receptive field. (d) **Copy-paste augmentation and oversampling** of small objects to address the training imbalance. (e) **Tiling/sliding-window inference** at test time — process overlapping high-resolution crops and merge detections. (f) **Better label assignment** that does not penalize small-object predictions as harshly for spatial misalignment (e.g., using center-sampling or task-aligned assignment).

For autonomy specifically, small-object detection often means detecting distant objects — pedestrians at 100m+, traffic signs at range, debris on the highway. The problem compounds with sensor limitations: at distance, even the raw camera signal may be just a few pixels. Multi-scale testing, super-resolution preprocessing, and crop-based detection (running a high-resolution crop around the vanishing point) are practical techniques used in production AV stacks. Dedicated small-object detection heads or attention mechanisms that focus on high-resolution features (like QueryDet's cascade query mechanism) can improve recall without the full cost of processing the entire image at high resolution.

### 4. How would you improve traffic-light and traffic-sign detection in the long tail?

Traffic-light and traffic-sign detection suffer from extreme long-tail distributions: a few common signs (stop, speed limit 30/50/65) dominate the training data, while rare signs (construction-specific, regional variants, damaged/occluded signs) appear only a handful of times. Traffic lights have the added complexity of state variation (solid, arrow, flashing, protected turn, pedestrian signals) and appearance variation (LED vs. incandescent, horizontal vs. vertical, different housings). The baseline failure mode is that models become confident on common classes and either misclassify or miss rare variants entirely.

Technical approaches that help: (a) **Hierarchical classification** — decompose the problem into detection (light/sign vs. background) and fine-grained classification (what kind), training each with appropriate loss weighting. This ensures rare classes at least get detected even if classification is uncertain. (b) **Balanced sampling and class-balanced loss** (e.g., effective number of samples weighting from Cui et al.) to prevent rare classes from being overwhelmed in the loss. (c) **Synthetic data augmentation** — traffic signs and lights are rigid, flat objects with known geometry, making them ideal candidates for copy-paste augmentation and synthetic rendering. You can render rare signs at various scales, orientations, and lighting conditions. (d) **Few-shot and metric learning** — learn an embedding space where novel sign types can be recognized from a few examples, rather than requiring hundreds of training samples per class.

The data-centric approach is often more impactful than model changes: (a) targeted data mining — search existing unlabeled fleet data for rare sign types using a weakly-supervised or self-supervised retrieval system, (b) active learning to prioritize labeling of underrepresented classes, (c) map priors — use HD maps to know where signs and lights are expected, reducing the detection problem to a confirmation/state-classification problem. For safety, the system should output calibrated uncertainty: it is better to flag an unknown sign for HD map lookup or human review than to confidently misclassify it. Maintaining a separate "novelty detection" head that identifies signs/lights that do not match any known class is a practical safety mechanism.

### 5. Why does NMS fail in crowded scenes, and what alternatives exist?

Non-Maximum Suppression (NMS) works by iteratively selecting the highest-confidence detection and suppressing all other detections that overlap with it above an IoU threshold. This fails in crowded scenes because the fundamental assumption — that high-IoU detections are duplicates — breaks down when ground-truth objects genuinely overlap. In a crowd of pedestrians, two adjacent people can have IoU > 0.5 between their bounding boxes, causing NMS to suppress a true positive. Lowering the IoU threshold catches more true positives but also keeps more false positives (duplicate detections of the same object). This is a fundamental dilemma: NMS uses a single geometric threshold to make a semantic decision (are these the same object or different objects?).

Alternatives: (a) **Soft-NMS** decays confidence scores instead of hard suppression, using a Gaussian or linear penalty based on IoU overlap. This is a drop-in replacement that helps in moderate crowding but does not fundamentally solve the problem. (b) **Matrix NMS** parallelizes the suppression computation for faster inference. (c) **Learned NMS / Relation Networks** — use a small network to predict whether two detections are duplicates, conditioning on features rather than just IoU. (d) **Set-based prediction (DETR family)** eliminates NMS entirely by using Hungarian matching during training to establish a one-to-one mapping between predictions and ground-truth objects. Each learned query predicts at most one object, so duplicate predictions are penalized during training and naturally avoided at inference.

For crowded-scene benchmarks (CrowdHuman, WiderPerson), DETR-style detectors and methods that predict a "repulsion" or "crowding" penalty (Repulsion Loss, AggLoss) show clear improvements. Repulsion Loss adds a term that pushes each predicted box away from neighboring ground-truth boxes that it does not match, encouraging tighter, more discriminative localization. In practice for autonomy, crowded pedestrian scenarios are safety-critical, and the NMS threshold becomes a tunable knob that trades off precision vs. recall — often requiring scene-dependent or density-dependent thresholds rather than a single global value.

### 6. How do classification confidence and localization quality get entangled?

Standard detection heads output a classification score and box coordinates independently, but NMS and evaluation metrics use the classification score to rank detections. This creates a problematic entanglement: a detection with high classification confidence but poor localization (offset or loose box) will be ranked above a detection with lower confidence but better localization. At evaluation time (mAP computation), this misranking directly hurts performance — the poorly-localized high-confidence box may match a ground truth at a low IoU threshold but fail at higher thresholds, and it prevents the better-localized box from being counted.

The root cause is that the classification head is trained to predict "is this an object?" while what NMS needs is "is this a good detection?" — a joint measure of class correctness and spatial precision. Several solutions address this: (a) **IoU-aware scoring** — train an auxiliary head to predict the IoU between the predicted box and the matched ground truth, then multiply the classification score by the IoU prediction at inference. This re-ranks detections by joint quality. (b) **FCOS centerness** — predicts how centered a prediction is within the ground-truth box, downweighting detections from box edges. (c) **Task-Aligned Learning (TTAL / TAL)** — jointly considers classification score and IoU in the label assignment, so the classification target itself encodes localization quality (soft labels proportional to IoU). (d) **Generalized Focal Loss (GFL)** merges classification and IoU prediction into a single unified representation, predicting a continuous quality score instead of a hard class label.

This entanglement is especially problematic for safety-critical systems because a confident-but-poorly-localized detection can cause a downstream planner to act on incorrect spatial information. A car detected with 0.95 confidence but 1 meter offset in 3D can cause a planner to brake unnecessarily or fail to yield. Separating localization quality from classification confidence — or unifying them into a single quality-aware score — is not just an accuracy improvement; it directly impacts downstream decision quality.

### 7. What is label assignment, and why does it matter so much?

Label assignment is the process of deciding which predicted anchors, points, or queries are responsible for detecting each ground-truth object during training. It determines the positive/negative split — which predictions receive a regression target and which are treated as background. This is arguably the most important design decision in a detector because it defines the training signal: poor assignment creates contradictory gradients (e.g., assigning a prediction that is far from the ground truth as positive, or suppressing a well-localized prediction as negative), directly degrading learning.

Classical assignment strategies are static and IoU-based: in Faster R-CNN, an anchor is positive if its IoU with any ground truth exceeds 0.7, negative if below 0.3. This is simple but suboptimal — it ignores the model's current predictions and can assign anchors that the model finds hard to learn from. Modern dynamic assignment strategies (OTA, SimOTA in YOLOX, Task-Aligned Assignment in YOLOv8) formulate assignment as an optimization problem: they consider both the classification confidence and localization quality of the model's current predictions when assigning labels. SimOTA uses a cost matrix combining classification and regression losses, then solves an approximate optimal transport problem to assign each ground truth its best-matched predictions. This creates a virtuous cycle: as the model improves, the assignment adapts, providing better training signal.

The impact is substantial — switching from static to dynamic assignment can improve mAP by 2-4 points without any architectural change. Task-Aligned Assignment (TAL) goes further by aligning the assignment metric with the final evaluation metric, computing an alignment score `t = s^alpha * u^beta` where `s` is classification score and `u` is IoU, then selecting the top-k predictions per ground truth as positives. This ensures the model is trained to produce predictions that score well under the actual ranking criterion used at test time. For dense prediction in robotics/autonomy, where the data distribution is highly imbalanced (most locations are background), the quality of label assignment directly determines training efficiency and final recall on rare object classes.

## Segmentation, Tracking, and Temporal Modeling

### 1. What changes when the metric is mIoU instead of mAP?

mIoU (mean Intersection over Union) is a per-pixel, per-class metric used in semantic segmentation, while mAP is a detection metric based on ranked predictions with confidence thresholds. The most important difference is that mIoU treats every pixel equally within each class, averaging IoU across classes. This creates fundamentally different optimization pressures: (a) there is no confidence ranking — every pixel must be assigned a class, so there is no notion of "suppressing low-confidence predictions," (b) class imbalance manifests differently — a class covering 1% of pixels (e.g., poles, traffic signs) has equal weight in the mean as a class covering 50% (road), making rare-class performance critical, and (c) boundary precision matters enormously because boundary pixels have outsized impact on IoU for thin/small classes.

These differences change model design: segmentation models use dense prediction heads (per-pixel classification) rather than box heads, loss functions often include auxiliary boundary losses or class-balanced weighting, and architectures favor high-resolution feature maps (DeepLab's dilated convolutions, HRNet's multi-resolution branches). The mIoU metric's sensitivity to rare classes often drives the use of class-balanced cross-entropy or Lovasz-Softmax loss (a smooth surrogate for IoU that can be directly optimized). Data augmentation strategies also differ: for detection, augmentations that move bounding boxes are acceptable, but for segmentation, augmentations must precisely transform pixel labels.

For autonomy, the shift from mAP to mIoU represents a shift from "did we find the objects" to "do we understand the full scene." Segmentation errors at class boundaries can cause downstream planning failures — a few pixels of road misclassified as sidewalk can shift a planned trajectory. The mIoU metric does not capture this spatial coherence directly, which is why production systems often add boundary-specific metrics (boundary IoU, boundary F1) and instance-level evaluation (panoptic quality) to complement mIoU.

### 2. How do you think about temporal consistency in video models?

Temporal consistency means that model predictions for the same physical entity should be coherent across frames — a car detected in frame t should have a similar box, class, and identity in frame t+1, without random flickering, ID changes, or geometric jumps. The absence of temporal consistency creates two problems: (a) downstream consumers (trackers, planners) receive noisy inputs that require heavy filtering, and (b) inconsistencies can trigger false events (a flickering detection causes a phantom braking response).

There are several levels at which you can inject temporal consistency: (a) **Post-hoc smoothing** — apply Kalman filters, exponential moving averages, or tracklet-based voting on per-frame detections. This is simple but purely reactive and cannot recover information lost in individual frames. (b) **Feature-level temporal fusion** — aggregate features across frames before making predictions. This can be done via recurrent layers (ConvLSTM, GRU), temporal attention (BEVFormer's temporal self-attention), or feature warping using estimated ego-motion or optical flow. This gives the model access to multi-frame evidence, improving both accuracy (temporal context disambiguates occluded or ambiguous frames) and consistency (shared features produce smoother outputs). (c) **Loss-level enforcement** — add auxiliary losses that penalize prediction discontinuities between consecutive frames, or use video-level matching losses that enforce tracking consistency during training.

The key design tension is between temporal smoothing and temporal responsiveness. A model that heavily smooths across time will be consistent but slow to react to sudden events — a pedestrian stepping into the road, an obstacle appearing from occlusion. For safety-critical systems, the latency of temporal models (how many frames of history they need, how quickly they adapt to scene changes) must be carefully characterized. The practical approach is often hierarchical: fast per-frame detection with lightweight temporal smoothing for reactive safety, combined with longer-horizon temporal models for scene understanding and prediction.

### 3. What causes ID switches in tracking, and how do you reduce them?

ID switches occur when a tracking algorithm incorrectly reassigns an object's identity — object A's track gets assigned to object B, or a track is terminated and a new one created for the same object. The primary causes are: (a) **Occlusion** — when an object is occluded for several frames, its track may be killed, and upon reappearance, it gets a new ID. (b) **Appearance similarity** — in crowded scenes with similar-looking objects (e.g., multiple white sedans), the re-identification (ReID) features may not discriminate sufficiently. (c) **Association failures** — the cost matrix in the Hungarian matching or greedy assignment may produce incorrect matches when objects cross paths or move unpredictably. (d) **Detection instability** — if the detector misses an object for a frame or produces a spatially shifted detection, the association can break.

Reducing ID switches requires improvements across the full pipeline: (a) **Stronger ReID features** — train an appearance embedding that discriminates between similar objects, using contrastive or triplet losses on tracking datasets. (b) **Better motion models** — Kalman filters with constant-velocity assumptions fail during acceleration, turning, or stopping. Learned motion models or longer prediction horizons reduce the search space for association. (c) **Longer track memory** — instead of killing tracks after N frames of no match, maintain them in a "lost" state with continued motion prediction, allowing re-association when the object reappears. (d) **Cascaded association** — match high-confidence detections first, then attempt to match lower-confidence detections to remaining tracks (as in ByteTrack, which uses all detections including low-confidence ones). (e) **Tracklet interpolation and stitching** — post-processing that identifies fragmented tracklets of the same object and merges them.

For autonomous vehicles, ID switches are not just a metric problem — they directly affect prediction and planning. If a tracked vehicle's ID switches, its motion history is lost, and the prediction module cannot accurately forecast its trajectory. This can cause erratic planner behavior. Modern approaches like QDTrack (quasi-dense matching) and tracking-by-attention (using transformer decoders that maintain persistent object queries across frames) embed tracking into the detection architecture itself, reducing the disconnect between detection and association that causes many ID switches.

### 4. When is optical flow useful, and when is it dead weight?

Optical flow — dense per-pixel motion estimation between consecutive frames — is useful when you need explicit motion information and cannot get it from other sources. Classic use cases: (a) **Motion segmentation** — separating moving objects from static background without semantic labels. (b) **Feature warping** — aligning features from previous frames to the current frame to build temporal context (used in video detection and segmentation). (c) **Action recognition** — two-stream architectures used flow as an explicit motion stream alongside RGB. (d) **Scene flow** — combined with depth, optical flow gives 3D motion vectors useful for dynamic object reconstruction.

Optical flow becomes dead weight when: (a) the model can learn equivalent motion features implicitly from raw frame pairs — modern video transformers and 3D CNNs (SlowFast, Video Swin) learn temporal relationships without explicit flow, often outperforming two-stream approaches while being simpler. (b) The computational cost of flow estimation is not justified by the accuracy gain — high-quality flow (RAFT, FlowFormer) is expensive, and on latency-constrained systems, that compute budget is better spent on a larger detection backbone. (c) Flow quality degrades in the conditions where you need it most — textureless regions, occlusion boundaries, large displacements, and independent object motion all produce unreliable flow estimates, which then become noisy inputs to downstream models.

For autonomy, the trend has been away from explicit optical flow toward implicit temporal fusion. BEVFormer-style temporal attention and ego-motion-compensated feature warping provide the benefits of temporal alignment without the cost and failure modes of explicit flow estimation. However, optical flow remains useful for offline tasks like data mining (finding frames with interesting motion patterns in fleet data), visualization and debugging (understanding what the model should be seeing), and as a self-supervised pretraining signal (predicting flow between frames as a pretext task for learning motion-aware features).

### 5. How do you fuse temporal context without leaking labels or causing train-test mismatch?

Temporal fusion during training introduces a subtle but critical risk: if you use ground-truth labels or model predictions from adjacent frames as inputs during training, but these are not available or are different quality at test time, you create a train-test mismatch. The most common leakage: (a) **Teacher forcing with future frames** — if the model sees frame t+1 features during training to predict frame t, but at test time only has past frames, performance drops. (b) **Ground-truth history injection** — using ground-truth tracks or boxes from previous frames as conditioning during training, but using predicted tracks at test time (the classic exposure bias problem from sequence modeling). (c) **Temporal augmentation mismatch** — if temporal augmentation (frame dropping, jittering) is not applied during training, the model overfits to consistent frame intervals and breaks with real-world timing variation.

Safe temporal fusion strategies: (a) **Causal-only context** — only fuse features from past frames (t-1, t-2, ...), never future frames, matching the inference constraint. (b) **Predicted-from-predicted** — during training, use the model's own predictions for temporal context rather than ground truth. This is noisier but avoids the train-test distribution gap. Alternatively, use a scheduled mixing strategy (like scheduled sampling in seq2seq) that gradually transitions from ground-truth to predicted context. (c) **Feature-level fusion only** — fuse intermediate features from past frames rather than labels or detection outputs, since features are available at both train and test time. (d) **Explicit ego-motion compensation** — when warping past-frame features to the current frame, use the same ego-motion source (e.g., IMU/odometry) at train and test time, not ground-truth ego-pose at train time and estimated ego-pose at test time.

The diagnostic for temporal mismatch is simple but often overlooked: compute metrics with and without temporal context, and verify that the gap is consistent between validation and test. If temporal fusion improves validation metrics by 5% but test metrics by only 1%, you likely have leakage. For safety-critical systems, temporal fusion must be robust to dropped frames, variable frame rates, and sensor timing jitter — training with explicit temporal augmentation (random frame drops, asynchronous frame sampling) is essential for bridging the gap between idealized training sequences and messy real-world data streams.

## Losses, Optimization, and Training Dynamics

### 1. Explain focal loss, class-balanced sampling, hard-negative mining, and when each helps.

**Focal loss** (Lin et al., 2017) modifies cross-entropy by adding a modulating factor: FL(p_t) = -alpha_t * (1 - p_t)^gamma * log(p_t). When gamma > 0, easy examples (high p_t, i.e., the model is already confident) contribute exponentially less to the loss, focusing training on hard, misclassified examples. This was designed to address the extreme foreground-background imbalance in dense detectors (100k anchors, <100 positive) where the accumulated loss from easy negatives overwhelms the useful gradient from hard examples. Focal loss is most effective when the imbalance is extreme (>100:1) and the model can reach a state where most examples are easy but a minority of hard examples drive improvement.

**Class-balanced sampling** addresses class imbalance at the data level rather than the loss level: you resample or reweight batches so that rare classes appear more frequently during training. This can be uniform sampling (every class equally likely), square-root sampling (proportional to sqrt(frequency), a compromise), or effective-number-of-samples weighting (Cui et al., 2019), which models the diminishing marginal value of additional examples for frequent classes. Class-balanced sampling is most effective when the imbalance is at the class level (many-class long-tail distributions like LVIS) rather than foreground-background.

**Hard-negative mining** (used in SSD, originally in SVMs for face detection) explicitly selects the hardest negative examples to include in each training batch, typically by ranking negatives by loss and keeping the top-k. This focuses the model on the decision boundary. It is most effective when there are specific, identifiable hard negatives (e.g., background regions that look like objects) and when you want to directly improve precision by forcing the model to discriminate confusable examples. The downside is potential instability — training only on hard examples can cause oscillation if the model alternately memorizes and forgets different hard cases.

In practice, these methods are complementary but not always necessary together. Focal loss largely subsumes hard-negative mining for dense detectors (it is a smooth, continuous version of the same idea). Class-balanced sampling is orthogonal and addresses a different axis of imbalance (between object classes vs. between object and background). For long-tail recognition tasks (e.g., rare sign types), class-balanced sampling plus a standard loss often outperforms focal loss alone, because focal loss addresses easy-vs-hard but not rare-vs-frequent.

### 2. Why can cross-entropy keep decreasing while your real metric worsens?

Cross-entropy measures the average log-likelihood of the model's predicted probability for the correct class. It can decrease (model becomes more confident on training examples) even as the actual task metric (mAP, mIoU, accuracy on a balanced test set) degrades, for several reasons. First, **overfitting**: the model memorizes training examples, assigning increasingly extreme probabilities to training data while generalizing worse to unseen data. Cross-entropy on the training set drops monotonically, but test-set accuracy stalls or declines. Second, **calibration divergence**: the model can reduce cross-entropy by making already-correct predictions more confident (pushing p from 0.9 to 0.99) without improving accuracy — this is optimizing the loss without improving the metric, because accuracy only cares about argmax, not confidence magnitude.

Third, and more subtly, cross-entropy optimizes a per-sample objective while metrics like mAP involve ranking (the ordering of detections by confidence matters) and thresholding (predictions below a score threshold are discarded). A model that becomes over-confident on false positives can increase mAP at one operating point while reducing it at another, and the cross-entropy does not capture this ranking structure. Similarly, mIoU requires the model to get boundary pixels right, but cross-entropy weights all pixels equally by confidence — the model can reduce CE by becoming more confident on easy interior pixels while still misclassifying difficult boundary pixels.

The practical takeaway: always monitor the actual task metric during training, not just the loss. Use the task metric for model selection (early stopping, hyperparameter tuning). If you observe loss-metric divergence, common remedies include: (a) stronger regularization (dropout, weight decay, augmentation) to prevent overfitting, (b) label smoothing to prevent extreme confidence, (c) switching to a loss that better aligns with the metric (Lovasz loss for IoU, AP loss for mAP), and (d) class-balanced weighting if the metric weighs classes equally but the loss is dominated by frequent classes.

### 3. What causes gradient explosion or vanishing in modern deep nets, and how do you diagnose it?

Gradient vanishing occurs when gradients shrink exponentially as they propagate backward through many layers, causing early layers to receive near-zero updates and learn extremely slowly. Gradient explosion is the opposite — gradients grow exponentially, causing numerical overflow or erratic parameter updates. In classical networks, both stem from repeated multiplication of weight matrices and activation derivatives during backpropagation: if the spectral norm of the Jacobian at each layer is consistently < 1, gradients vanish; if > 1, they explode. Saturating activations (sigmoid, tanh) exacerbate vanishing because their derivatives are near zero for large inputs.

Modern deep nets largely mitigate these issues through: (a) **Residual connections** — skip connections add the identity to each block's Jacobian, keeping the effective Jacobian near 1 and providing a gradient highway to early layers. (b) **Normalization layers** (BatchNorm, LayerNorm, GroupNorm) — stabilize the forward activations, which indirectly stabilizes gradients. (c) **Careful initialization** (He, Xavier/Glorot) — sets initial weight magnitudes so that variance is preserved across layers. (d) **Non-saturating activations** (ReLU, GELU, SiLU) — gradients are 0 or 1 (for ReLU), avoiding the vanishing problem in the non-zero regime.

However, these issues still arise in modern nets: (a) **Very deep transformers** without proper pre-LayerNorm ordering can have gradient issues in the attention layers. (b) **Recurrent processing** or iterative refinement modules (e.g., RAFT's iterative flow refinement) accumulate gradients over many steps. (c) **Mixed-precision training** — FP16 has a limited dynamic range, and gradients can underflow to zero or overflow to inf; loss scaling is the standard fix. (d) **Large learning rates or poor warmup** schedules can cause transient gradient explosion early in training. **Diagnosis**: monitor gradient norms per layer over training steps (if they trend toward zero or infinity, you have a problem), check for NaN/Inf in loss or activations, visualize gradient flow through the network using tools like `torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_` with logging. A practical test: if zeroing out the gradients to the first 10% of layers does not change training dynamics, those layers are not learning (vanishing gradients).

### 4. How do label smoothing, temperature scaling, and calibration interact?

**Label smoothing** replaces hard one-hot targets y = [0, 0, 1, 0] with soft targets y = [epsilon/K, epsilon/K, 1-epsilon+epsilon/K, epsilon/K], where epsilon is typically 0.1 and K is the number of classes. This prevents the model from learning to output extreme logits (infinite magnitude) to minimize cross-entropy, acting as a regularizer on the logit space. It improves generalization and produces inherently softer probability distributions, which are often better calibrated — the model is less prone to extreme overconfidence. However, label smoothing changes the learned representation: it encourages class clusters in penultimate-layer feature space to be equidistant (a simplex structure), which can hurt knowledge distillation or feature-based downstream tasks.

**Temperature scaling** is a post-hoc calibration method: you divide logits by a learned scalar T before softmax. T > 1 softens the distribution (reduces confidence), T < 1 sharpens it. The temperature is typically learned on a held-out calibration set by minimizing negative log-likelihood. Temperature scaling does not change the model's predictions (argmax is invariant to positive scaling), only the confidence values. This makes it a clean, orthogonal calibration tool that can be applied after training without affecting accuracy.

The interaction: label smoothing during training produces models that are generally less overconfident but may be miscalibrated in domain-specific ways (e.g., uniformly under-confident). Temperature scaling can then be applied post-hoc to fine-tune calibration on the target distribution. They are complementary but operate at different stages — label smoothing is a training-time regularizer that affects learned representations, while temperature scaling is a post-hoc fix that only adjusts output confidence. For safety-critical perception, calibration is essential because downstream modules (prediction, planning) use confidence scores to weight evidence and make risk-sensitive decisions. A model that reports 0.99 confidence should be correct 99% of the time. The Expected Calibration Error (ECE) metric measures this, and the standard recipe is: train with label smoothing for regularization, then apply temperature scaling on a calibration set for final calibration.

### 5. When would you change the loss before changing the model?

You should change the loss before the model when the model has sufficient capacity to solve the task but is being trained toward the wrong objective. This is more common than people think. Classic examples: (a) using L2 regression loss for bounding box coordinates when the error distribution is heavy-tailed (L1 or Smooth-L1 is more robust to outlier annotations), (b) using vanilla cross-entropy for a long-tailed dataset where 90% of the gradient comes from the head classes (switching to class-balanced or focal loss immediately helps), (c) using a pixel-wise loss for segmentation when the metric is IoU (Lovasz-Softmax loss directly optimizes a surrogate for IoU and often gives 1-2 point mIoU improvement with zero architectural change).

The diagnostic heuristic: if the model can overfit the training set (training metric is good) but validation metrics are poor, the issue is likely generalization (change regularization or data, not the loss). If the model cannot achieve good training metrics despite having enough capacity, the loss may be misaligned with the task. Also change the loss first when: (a) there is a known metric-loss mismatch (you care about ranking but use pointwise loss), (b) training is numerically unstable (switch from CE to focal loss, or from L2 to Huber), (c) you need to incorporate task-specific structure (GIoU/DIoU loss for box regression encodes geometric properties that L1 does not).

Changing the loss is almost always cheaper than changing the model: it requires no architectural changes, preserves existing infrastructure (data pipelines, deployment code), and typically adds minimal computational overhead. In a production ML system, loss changes are low-risk experiments — you can A/B test different losses on the same architecture and data within hours. Model changes require rewriting code, revalidating deployment latency, and potentially re-qualifying the entire system. The loss should be the first knob you turn after checking data quality.

### 6. How do you debug a training curve that looks healthy but produces nonsense visualizations?

This is a deceptively common failure mode: the loss decreases smoothly, validation metrics look reasonable, but when you visualize actual model outputs (predicted boxes, segmentation masks, depth maps), they are clearly wrong. The most common causes:

**Evaluation bug**: the metric computation is incorrect — using the wrong IoU threshold, not matching predictions to ground truths correctly, or computing the metric on a subset of the data that happens to look good. This is the most common cause and the first thing to check. Recompute metrics manually on a small set of examples where you can verify by hand.

**Label mismatch**: the model is predicting labels in a different format, coordinate system, or class ordering than the visualization assumes. For example, the model predicts boxes in (cx, cy, w, h) normalized coordinates but the visualization code expects (x1, y1, x2, y2) pixel coordinates. Or class indices are shifted by 1 (0-indexed vs. 1-indexed). The loss can still decrease because the model learns to map inputs to labels — it just learns a different mapping than you expect. **Data pipeline bug**: training data and visualization data go through different preprocessing pipelines, or augmentations are applied to images but not labels (or vice versa). The model learns the training distribution but that distribution is corrupted. Check: visualize the actual training batch (input + target) as the model sees it, not the raw data.

**Metric gaming**: the model finds a degenerate solution that scores well on the metric but is not useful. For example, a segmentation model that predicts everything as "road" can achieve a reasonable overall accuracy (roads cover most pixels) even though it is useless. This is why class-balanced metrics (mIoU) and per-class breakdowns are essential — the headline number can mask catastrophic failure on specific classes. The debugging recipe: (1) visualize training batches with targets, (2) visualize model predictions on training data (should overfit well), (3) visualize per-class metrics, (4) manually verify the metric computation on 5-10 examples.

## Data and Evaluation

### 1. What is a good slice strategy for autonomy or robotics data?

A "slice" is a semantically meaningful subset of the data, and a slice strategy defines how you partition the data to understand model performance beyond aggregate metrics. For autonomy, good slicing axes include: (a) **Scene conditions** — time of day (dawn, day, dusk, night), weather (clear, rain, fog, snow, glare), lighting (direct sun, shadows, headlights, tunnel transitions). (b) **Scenario type** — highway, urban, suburban, intersection, parking lot, construction zone. (c) **Object properties** — distance (near/mid/far), size (small/medium/large), occlusion level (0-25%, 25-50%, 50-75%, 75%+), object type (car, truck, pedestrian, cyclist, animal). (d) **Ego behavior** — straight driving, turning, lane changing, stopped, accelerating/decelerating. (e) **Complexity** — number of road users, traffic density, number of simultaneous events.

The key principle is that slices should correspond to failure modes, not just data attributes. A slice like "nighttime pedestrians at >50m on unlit roads" is more actionable than just "nighttime" because it isolates a specific, safety-critical failure mode with a clear remediation path (more data of this type, sensor improvements, specialized model heads). The slice strategy should be hierarchical: start with coarse slices (day/night) to find broad patterns, then drill into fine slices (night + rain + pedestrian + far) to isolate root causes.

For robotics, additional slicing dimensions include: manipulation task phase (approach, grasp, transport, place), object material properties (rigid, deformable, transparent, reflective), workspace geometry (cluttered, sparse, confined), and robot state (joint limits, singularities, high-speed vs. precision mode). The slice strategy should be version-controlled and evaluated automatically in CI: every model candidate should be assessed on all defined slices, with alerts when any slice degrades beyond a threshold.

### 2. How do you audit annotation quality beyond spot checks?

Systematic annotation quality auditing uses statistical and model-based methods to catch errors at scale: (a) **Inter-annotator agreement** — have multiple annotators label the same examples and measure agreement (Cohen's kappa for classification, IoU for boxes/masks). Low agreement indicates ambiguous guidelines, hard examples, or inconsistent annotators. Disagreement maps reveal which regions or classes are most problematic. (b) **Model-based outlier detection** — train a model on the existing annotations, then flag examples where the model's prediction strongly disagrees with the annotation. These are either annotation errors (model is right, label is wrong) or genuinely hard examples (model is wrong). Manual review of these flagged examples efficiently finds systematic errors.

(c) **Annotation consistency checks** — automated rules that catch structural errors: bounding boxes outside image boundaries, zero-area masks, overlapping instances with identical class labels, labels that violate physical constraints (a traffic light 1 pixel tall, a car annotated in the sky). (d) **Temporal consistency** for video — track annotations across frames and flag sudden jumps in box position, size, or class. If an object is labeled as "car" for 50 frames and "truck" for 1 frame, the single frame is likely an error. (e) **Confident learning** (Cleanlab-style) — estimate the joint distribution P(noisy label, true label) from model predictions, identifying which examples are likely mislabeled based on their position relative to the model's decision boundary.

For safety-critical applications, annotation quality auditing should be a continuous process, not a one-time check. Establish an annotation error rate target (e.g., <1% for safety-critical classes like pedestrians), measure it through systematic sampling, and track it over time. Feed annotation quality signals back to the labeling pipeline: retrain annotators on common error patterns, refine annotation guidelines for ambiguous cases, and escalate hard examples to senior annotators or domain experts. The cost of annotation auditing is small compared to the cost of training on bad labels.

### 3. How do you decide whether to collect more data or relabel existing data?

The decision depends on diagnosing whether the performance bottleneck is data coverage or data quality. **Collect more data** when: (a) error analysis shows failure modes in scenarios that are absent or severely underrepresented in the training set (e.g., no snow examples, no nighttime pedestrians), (b) the model is underfitting on specific slices — it has not seen enough diversity to learn robust features, (c) the model shows high variance across random seeds or cross-validation folds, suggesting insufficient data for stable learning. In these cases, relabeling existing data does not help because the underlying signal is missing.

**Relabel existing data** when: (a) annotation auditing reveals high error rates — a model trained on cleaner labels would immediately improve. A rough rule of thumb: if >5% of labels in a critical class are wrong, relabeling will likely help more than collecting more noisy data. (b) Annotation guidelines have evolved — the definition of classes, boundary conventions, or edge-case handling has changed, making old labels inconsistent with new ones. (c) The model's errors on a held-out set are correlated with annotation inconsistencies — for example, the model consistently predicts a different boundary than the annotation, and manual inspection shows the model is often right.

The pragmatic approach: (a) Run confident learning or model-based error detection to estimate the annotation error rate. (b) Compute a "data coverage" heat map across your slicing dimensions — identify gaps. (c) If both are problematic, prioritize relabeling first (it is usually cheaper and faster than data collection), then collect data to fill coverage gaps. For autonomy, there is a third option often overlooked: **mine existing fleet logs** for underrepresented scenarios, then label those specifically. This is cheaper than targeted data collection and fills coverage gaps more efficiently than random data accumulation.

### 4. What is the difference between IID validation, scenario-based validation, and operational validation?

**IID validation** is the standard ML setup: split data randomly into train/validation/test sets, assuming all splits are drawn from the same distribution. This measures how well the model generalizes to new samples from the same distribution. IID validation is necessary but insufficient for safety-critical systems because real-world deployment rarely matches the IID assumption — specific scenarios, geographic regions, sensor configurations, or time periods can be systematically different from the training distribution.

**Scenario-based validation** evaluates the model on curated sets of specific scenarios, often designed to test known edge cases, regulatory requirements, or safety-critical situations. For autonomous vehicles, this might include: pedestrian crossing at night, emergency vehicle approaching, construction zone navigation, sensor degradation (rain-covered lens, sun glare). Each scenario becomes a test case with explicit pass/fail criteria. This is closer to software testing than statistical evaluation — you are verifying specific behaviors, not just aggregate performance. Scenario-based validation catches the "long tail" failures that IID validation misses because rare scenarios appear in IID test sets too infrequently to provide statistical power.

**Operational validation** evaluates the model under conditions that match the actual deployment environment: the specific sensor hardware, compute platform, geographic region, time of year, and operational design domain (ODD). This includes closed-course testing, shadow-mode evaluation (running the model in parallel with a safety driver), and real-world deployment metrics. Operational validation captures system-level effects that neither IID nor scenario-based validation can: latency variation under thermal throttling, sensor calibration drift, interaction effects between perception and downstream modules. For a complete validation strategy, you need all three: IID validation for basic model quality, scenario-based validation for edge-case coverage, and operational validation for deployment readiness. They form a pyramid — IID is cheap and broad, scenarios are more expensive but targeted, operational is the most expensive but most truthful.

### 5. How do you detect when a benchmark is no longer measuring the thing you care about?

Benchmark saturation and misalignment manifest in several diagnostic patterns: (a) **Metric ceiling effects** — when the state-of-the-art exceeds 95-98% on a benchmark, remaining improvements are often driven by test-set-specific tricks rather than genuine capability improvements. If marginal gains on the benchmark do not correspond to improvement on your actual deployment task, the benchmark has decoupled from your objective. (b) **Benchmark-specific overfitting** — if a model that ranks #1 on the benchmark performs worse than a lower-ranked model on your internal evaluation, the benchmark is no longer a reliable proxy. This often happens when the benchmark's data distribution, annotation style, or evaluation protocol diverges from your deployment requirements.

(c) **Construct validity failure** — the benchmark measures a proxy for what you actually care about, and the proxy relationship has broken. For example, ImageNet accuracy was a good proxy for visual feature quality until ~2020; beyond that point, ImageNet-specific architecture tricks (resolution tricks, heavy augmentation, test-time augmentation) improved ImageNet numbers without improving downstream transfer. Similarly, KITTI was a useful AV benchmark until the field outgrew its small size, limited diversity, and specific sensor configuration — improvements on KITTI stopped predicting improvements on real-world driving performance.

Detection strategies: (a) regularly cross-validate benchmark performance against internal evaluation on deployment-representative data, (b) track the correlation between benchmark improvements and deployment metric improvements over time — if the correlation weakens, the benchmark is losing relevance, (c) examine whether top benchmark methods use techniques that would not be applicable in your deployment setting (test-time augmentation, model ensembles, resolution tricks), (d) check whether the benchmark's class distribution, scene diversity, and annotation quality match your deployment needs. When you detect misalignment, the right response is to build an internal benchmark that better represents your deployment requirements, while still tracking public benchmarks for community comparison.

### 6. What metrics would you report for a safety-critical perception model besides the headline metric?

Beyond the headline metric (mAP, mIoU, or similar), safety-critical perception requires a comprehensive metric suite: (a) **Per-class breakdown** — aggregate metrics hide per-class variation; a model with 80% mAP might have 95% AP on cars and 40% AP on pedestrians, and the pedestrian performance is what matters for safety. (b) **Performance at operating points** — precision-recall curves, not just AP. The system operates at a specific threshold, and performance at that threshold (precision, recall, F1) is more relevant than area under the curve. Report recall at fixed precision levels (e.g., "at 99.9% precision, recall is X%"). (c) **Distance-stratified metrics** — detection performance at 0-30m, 30-60m, 60-100m, 100m+. Safety requirements scale differently with distance; miss rates at close range are more dangerous than at long range.

(d) **Latency and throughput** — perception latency directly affects the system's reaction time. Report p50, p95, and p99 latency, not just average, because worst-case latency matters more than average for safety. (e) **Calibration metrics** — Expected Calibration Error (ECE), reliability diagrams. Overconfident false positives and underconfident true positives both cause downstream problems. (f) **Failure mode analysis** — false positive rate by type (what gets falsely detected?), false negative rate by scenario (what gets missed?). (g) **Robustness metrics** — performance under domain shift (weather, lighting, sensor degradation), adversarial perturbations, or input corruption (ImageNet-C style).

(h) **Temporal stability** — jitter in detections across frames (box position variance, confidence variance), tracking consistency metrics (MOTA, IDF1). (i) **Safety-specific metrics** — time-to-detect (how early before a collision event is the object first detected?), minimum detectable size (what is the smallest object reliably detected?), detection in safety-critical zones (ego-lane, crosswalks). The metric report should also include worst-case performance across defined slices: the overall average can improve while specific critical slices degrade, and the system is only as safe as its weakest slice in the operational domain.

### 7. How do you build a useful error taxonomy?

An error taxonomy categorizes model failures into actionable groups, each with a different root cause and remediation path. The taxonomy should be hierarchical: top-level categories distinguish between error types (false positives, false negatives, misclassifications, mislocalization), and sub-levels decompose each by cause (data issue, annotation issue, model limitation, distribution shift, sensor artifact). A useful taxonomy has three properties: (a) **Mutually exclusive** — every error belongs to exactly one category, (b) **Collectively exhaustive** — no errors are uncategorized, (c) **Actionable** — each category maps to a specific remediation (collect more data, fix annotations, change architecture, add augmentation, adjust loss function).

The process: (a) start by manually reviewing 200-500 errors sampled from the validation set, covering all error types proportionally. (b) Inductively group errors by visual similarity and root cause — do not start with predefined categories; let the data reveal the taxonomy. (c) Formalize categories with clear definitions and visual examples. (d) Have multiple reviewers categorize the same errors independently to verify inter-rater agreement on the taxonomy definitions. (e) Automate coarse-level categorization where possible (e.g., false positive vs. false negative is automatically determined; "caused by occlusion" can be approximated by checking if the object was partially overlapped in the image).

For autonomy, the error taxonomy should connect perception errors to downstream impact: (a) "benign" errors that do not affect planning (e.g., misdetecting a tree as a pole — both are static obstacles with similar avoidance behavior), (b) "annoying" errors that degrade user experience but not safety (e.g., phantom braking from false positive detections), (c) "dangerous" errors that could lead to safety events (e.g., missed pedestrian in the ego path). This severity stratification ensures that model improvement efforts focus on the errors that matter most. The taxonomy should be a living document: as the model improves and new failure modes emerge, the taxonomy evolves. Review and update it every model iteration, and track the distribution of errors across categories over time to verify that improvements address the right failure modes.

## Robotics / 3D / Multimodal Extensions

### 1. What are the core bottlenecks in BEV-style perception systems?

BEV (Bird's Eye View) perception systems project camera features into a 3D voxel or BEV grid, enabling unified spatial reasoning across multiple cameras. The core bottlenecks are: (a) **Depth estimation accuracy** — the 2D-to-3D lifting step (as in LSS/BEVDet) requires predicting per-pixel depth distributions from monocular images. Depth errors directly translate to spatial misalignment in BEV space. At range, small angular errors produce large metric errors (a 0.5-degree error at 100m is ~0.9m of displacement). This is the fundamental limitation of camera-only BEV: unlike LiDAR, there is no direct depth measurement, and learned depth is inherently uncertain.

(b) **Computational cost** — constructing the BEV representation requires processing multiple camera views (typically 6 for surround-view), lifting features into a 3D volume, and performing 3D convolutions or BEV-space attention. This is memory-intensive (the 3D voxel grid can be large) and compute-intensive (3D convolutions are expensive). The resolution trade-off is severe: higher BEV resolution improves spatial precision but quadratically increases memory. Efficient approximations (sparse voxels, deformable attention, reduced Z-resolution) are necessary for real-time operation. (c) **Temporal alignment** — fusing BEV features across time requires accurate ego-motion estimation to warp past BEV grids into the current frame. Ego-motion errors accumulate and compound with depth errors, creating ghosting or duplication artifacts in the temporal BEV. (d) **Camera calibration sensitivity** — the projection from image to BEV depends critically on camera intrinsics and extrinsics. Any calibration drift (from vibration, temperature changes, or physical impacts) distorts the BEV geometry.

The practical consequence for AV stacks: BEV systems offer an elegant unified representation but introduce fragilities that component-based architectures (separate 2D detectors + 3D lifting) avoid. The trend toward transformer-based BEV (BEVFormer, StreamPETR) addresses some computational bottlenecks via sparse attention but introduces its own challenges: learned spatial cross-attention can attend to incorrect image regions, and attention-based lifting is harder to interpret and debug than explicit geometric projection.

---

### 2. How do camera calibration errors show up in downstream predictions?

Camera calibration encompasses intrinsic parameters (focal length, principal point, distortion coefficients) and extrinsic parameters (rotation and translation relative to the vehicle/robot body frame). Errors in these parameters propagate through every stage of perception that converts between image coordinates and 3D world coordinates.

**Intrinsic errors** cause: (a) incorrect mapping from pixel coordinates to bearing angles — objects appear at wrong angular positions, causing 3D position errors that scale with distance. A 1% focal length error at 100m produces ~1m lateral position error. (b) Distortion model errors cause spatially varying position errors, worst at image edges — objects in the periphery are displaced while center objects are accurate, creating inconsistent behavior that is hard to diagnose. (c) For depth-based BEV lifting, incorrect intrinsics mean the depth bins map to wrong physical distances, warping the entire BEV representation.

**Extrinsic errors** cause: (a) systematic spatial bias in all 3D predictions — if the camera is estimated 2cm too far left, every 3D detection is 2cm off, creating consistent but incorrect positioning. (b) Multi-camera fusion misalignment — in surround-view systems, extrinsic errors between cameras cause objects at camera boundaries to be duplicated or lost. A car at the boundary between front and left cameras may appear as two detections or zero detections depending on how the overlap zone is handled. (c) Ground plane misestimation — extrinsic errors that tilt the camera slightly cause the estimated ground plane to diverge from reality at range, making objects appear to float or sink.

The insidious aspect is that calibration errors often cause silent failures: the model's confidence may be high (it sees the object clearly in image space), but the 3D position is wrong. Downstream planning receives a high-confidence, incorrectly-positioned detection and plans accordingly. Calibration monitoring in production — comparing projected LiDAR points to image features, tracking landmark reprojection errors, or using cross-camera consistency checks — is essential for catching drift before it causes downstream failures.

### 3. What makes multimodal fusion brittle in practice?

Multimodal fusion (camera + LiDAR, camera + radar, RGB + depth, vision + language) promises complementary strengths but introduces several practical failure modes: (a) **Sensor synchronization** — modalities are captured at different times. At 60 km/h, a 50ms time offset between camera and LiDAR corresponds to ~0.8m of displacement. If fusion assumes synchronized inputs, this temporal misalignment creates phantom features — LiDAR points from one moment fused with camera features from another. Ego-motion compensation helps but is imperfect, especially during rapid maneuvers.

(b) **Calibration dependency** — fusing camera and LiDAR requires precise spatial calibration (the extrinsic transformation between sensors). As discussed above, calibration drift is common, and fusion methods that rely on projecting LiDAR points onto camera images (BEVFusion-style paint operations) are particularly sensitive. A calibration error means LiDAR points are painted with features from the wrong image region, providing actively misleading multimodal information. (c) **Modality failure** — when one sensor degrades or fails (camera overexposure, LiDAR return dropouts in rain, radar false positives from clutter), a tightly-fused system may produce worse outputs than a single-modality system, because it treats the corrupted modality as trustworthy. Late fusion and modality dropout during training help but do not fully solve this.

(d) **Feature alignment** — different modalities have different characteristics (camera features are dense, semantic, and spatially detailed; LiDAR features are sparse, geometric, and distance-accurate; radar is even sparser but velocity-aware). Finding the right representation space for fusion is an open problem. Early fusion (concatenating raw inputs) forces the model to learn alignment from scratch. Late fusion (combining per-modality predictions) loses cross-modal interaction. Mid-level feature fusion (BEVFusion, TransFusion) is the current best practice but requires careful architectural design to handle the density and dimensionality mismatch between modalities. The practical recommendation: design the system to degrade gracefully — it should work reasonably with any single modality and improve with additional modalities, rather than requiring all modalities to function correctly.

### 4. How would you reason about asynchronous sensors during training and evaluation?

Real sensor suites are asynchronous: a 30 Hz camera, 10 Hz LiDAR, and 20 Hz radar produce measurements at different times. Even nominally synchronized sensors have hardware jitter (capture timestamps vary by milliseconds). Training and evaluation must handle this asynchrony rather than assuming idealized synchronized inputs.

During training, you must decide how to pair measurements. Common approaches: (a) **Nearest-neighbor pairing** — for each reference timestamp (usually the LiDAR sweep or a fixed clock), find the closest measurement from each sensor. This is simple but introduces temporal misalignment that varies per sample. (b) **Interpolation** — interpolate between adjacent measurements to estimate what each sensor would have measured at the reference time. For LiDAR point clouds, this means motion-compensating points using ego-motion. For camera, this may mean accepting the nearest frame since image interpolation is not meaningful. (c) **Explicit time encoding** — feed the timestamp offset between modalities as an additional input, letting the model learn to compensate. This is important because a 10ms offset at 10 km/h is negligible, but at 120 km/h it is ~0.3m — the model needs to know the ego speed to interpret the offset correctly.

During evaluation, the key concern is that your evaluation data reflects real asynchrony rather than artificially synchronized inputs. If you evaluate on perfectly synchronized data but deploy with real sensor jitter, you overestimate performance. The evaluation protocol should: (a) use real sensor timestamps, not synthetic synchronization, (b) measure performance sensitivity to timing offsets (by artificially varying the timestamp alignment in evaluation), (c) report performance at realistic operating points (e.g., "with up to 50ms LiDAR-camera offset, mAP drops by X%"). For safety-critical systems, worst-case asynchrony (e.g., maximum expected timing jitter plus ego-motion during that interval) should be an explicit test case.

### 5. What kinds of pseudo-label errors are especially dangerous in autonomy stacks?

Pseudo-labels (generated by a teacher model, auto-labeling pipeline, or heuristic rules rather than human annotators) are increasingly used to scale up AV training data. While overall pseudo-label quality can be high (>95% precision), specific error patterns are disproportionately dangerous because they are systematic, not random, and concentrate in safety-critical scenarios.

The most dangerous pseudo-label errors: (a) **False negatives on vulnerable road users** — if the teacher model misses a pedestrian, cyclist, or motorcyclist, the student model is trained to not detect that object. Unlike random annotation noise (which the model can learn to average out), systematic false negatives from the teacher create blind spots that the student inherits and potentially amplifies. These errors correlate with difficulty — the teacher is most likely to miss objects in exactly the scenarios where detection matters most (occlusion, nighttime, unusual appearance). (b) **Spatially biased false positives** — if the teacher consistently hallucinates objects in certain image regions (e.g., due to lens flare patterns, bridge shadows, or radar clutter), the student learns these hallucinations as ground truth, creating systematic phantom detections that trigger false braking or evasive maneuvers.

(c) **Incorrect velocity/motion labels** — for tracking-based pseudo-labels, assigning the wrong velocity to an object can train a model to predict incorrect future trajectories. A parked car pseudo-labeled as moving (or vice versa) distorts the prediction module's training signal. (d) **Class confusion between safety-relevant classes** — misclassifying a pedestrian as a pole, or a motorcycle as a bicycle, changes the downstream behavior (different speed profiles, different interaction predictions). (e) **Temporal inconsistency** — pseudo-labels that flicker (object detected in frame 1, missed in frame 2, detected in frame 3) train the model to produce inconsistent outputs, which downstream temporal smoothing may or may not compensate for. Mitigation: filter pseudo-labels by confidence, cross-validate with multiple teachers, use human review for safety-critical scenarios, and always evaluate on human-labeled test sets to detect systematic biases inherited from the teacher.

### 6. How do long-tail safety cases distort your model-development priorities?

Long-tail safety cases — rare but high-consequence scenarios like a child running into the road, an emergency vehicle approaching from behind, debris in the lane, or a construction worker directing traffic — create a tension between statistical optimization and safety engineering. Standard ML development optimizes for aggregate metrics that are dominated by common scenarios: highway driving, following the car ahead, stopping at traffic lights. Rare safety cases contribute negligibly to aggregate metrics, so model improvements on rare cases do not move the headline number, making them invisible in standard evaluation.

This distortion manifests in several ways: (a) **Resource misallocation** — engineering effort flows toward improving the headline metric (because it is measurable and shows progress) rather than toward rare cases that matter more for safety. (b) **Architecture bias** — model architectures and loss functions are tuned for common scenarios, and design choices that help common cases can hurt rare cases (e.g., NMS thresholds tuned for sparse highway traffic fail in dense urban pedestrian crowds). (c) **Data collection bias** — cost-per-mile data collection naturally accumulates common scenarios; rare cases require expensive targeted collection or simulation. (d) **Evaluation blindness** — with 100 test examples of a rare scenario, you cannot reliably measure a 1% improvement, so you cannot tell if your changes helped.

The organizational remedy is to separate model development into two tracks: (a) an aggregate-metric track that optimizes for overall performance on common scenarios, using standard ML workflows, and (b) a safety-case track that defines explicit, enumerated scenarios with pass/fail criteria, invests in scenario-specific test sets (supplemented by simulation), and evaluates model candidates against these criteria independently of the headline metric. The safety-case track should have veto power: a model that improves aggregate metrics but regresses on a critical safety scenario should not be deployed. This requires organizational discipline because it means accepting lower aggregate metrics to maintain safety case coverage. Tools that help: scenario-based testing frameworks, importance-weighted evaluation that upweights rare scenarios, and simulation-augmented test suites that generate enough rare-case examples for statistical significance.
